# What We Know, What We Don't

*Seventh paper in the Concordius series, and the close of Section I. It introduces nothing new. It gathers what the foregoing papers established, and states the one thing they could not.*

---

## Abstract

This paper summarizes Section I. The constitutive arguments began from one assumption and drew a great deal out of it. The summary below sets those results back in order, briefly, and then returns to the assumption underneath them all — that reality can be modeled as a [[Appendix I - Dictionary of Terms#Gelfand Triple|Gelfand triple]] — and observes that this first premise is the one claim in the whole series that cannot itself be established. Everything after it was argued. It was assumed. The section ends where it began, holding the same premise it started with, and no more certain of it.

## 1. The argument, in brief

Section I made a single assumption: that reality can be modeled as a Gelfand triple — a space of well-behaved states held within a larger space of all states, held in turn within a still larger space of pure possibility, with one relation, the [[Appendix I - Dictionary of Terms#Inner Product|inner product]], running through all three. From that one premise **Paper 1** drew out a complete structure, and showed that the relation at its center is not a thing within the space but what the space is made of.

**Paper 2** added the one fact no one can sincerely deny — that there is someone here to doubt — and used it to put names to the structure: the relation that constitutes everything; the ordered domain of meaning that proceeds from it; the coherence that holds the two together. Read as this series reads them, these are the Father, the Son, and the Spirit, recognized in the mathematics rather than laid over it.

**Paper 3** turned from the act of making to what is made — the countless particular outcomes drawn into one coherent world — and found, along the way, that the constituting relation is what we mean, at bottom, by love: not something the ground feels, but what the ground is. To exist at all is to be held by it.

Then the argument went down. **Paper 3½** followed the structure as it thickens into constraint, level upon level, away from openness and toward fixity. **Paper 4** located the point where that descent becomes the physical world we live in — where, in about the most literal sense physics allows, the word becomes flesh. And for one paper, **Paper 5**, the framework set itself aside entirely and let the natural sciences tell the long story of that flesh: the forging of the elements and the planet, the first life, and the slow climb through living things to the last animal that stood just below us.

At the top of that climb the argument found us. **Paper 6** marked the threshold at which matter becomes a creature that can attend, and be indwelt, and begin the patient work of turning toward the ground rather than away from it — and set that creature climbing. Its companion, **Paper 6½**, traced the same path in the other direction: the ground coming down to meet the climb, the whole way down, so that the steps no one can take alone would be taken with help. Down by gift, up by work, the two meeting somewhere on the road.

That is Section I. One assumption, and a world unfolded from it.

## 2. The one thing we assumed

It is worth being exact about what has, and has not, been shown.

Almost everything above was argued. Granted the single premise, the rest followed — some of it with the force of derivation, some by strong inference, some by the convergence of independent witnesses, each marked as such where it stood. A reader is entitled to check every step, and is meant to.

But the premise itself was never argued. It was assumed on the first line of the first paper and never earned: *that reality can be modeled as a Gelfand triple.* Everything rests on it, and it rests on nothing further. There is no vantage outside the structure from which to confirm that reality really has this shape, because any confirmation would already be made from within it, using the very relation whose reality is the thing in question. The one claim the whole series most depends on is the one claim it can least support.

This is not a flaw to be repaired. It is where an argument of this kind has to end. A structure can describe everything it contains and still not reach the ground it stands on; the ground is presupposed by every description and captured by none. We have spent the section coming to know the place, and arrived, at the end, back at the one thing we began by taking on trust.

So the section closes as it opened — with a single assumption, neither proven nor abandoned, only better understood for everything that follows from it. We know a great deal, if it is true. Whether it is true is the one thing we do not, and from here cannot, know.

What can be done with a structure like this, once it is granted — and what happens when the same honest limit is met from the inside — is the work of the sections that follow.
