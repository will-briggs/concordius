# Benjamin Franklin: The Errata and the Fruit

*A Section III reading — one individual, read through the framework. Franklin is our personal **La**: the third bivector, theology × science — the empirical method turned not only on nature but on the moral self, and the virtue is **diligence**, industry personified. His life is set out here as a seven-note octave with its two shocks, the same treatment given to Joan, as a second test of the holographic content principle. Joan tested it in a life held true under fire; Franklin tests it in a different and harder case — a long, fruitful, and genuinely flawed life. We do not claim he was perfect. We do not claim any of these people are perfect. We look at the fruit. Companion to Joan of Arc (the Fa reading), Do-Tonic (which opens the readings), and La (the method this life enacts).*

*Status: a structural reading. The history is held as history, per the standard sources; the octave-structuring and the identification of Franklin with La are the reading, offered as concordance. The failings are stated plainly and kept fair — neither inflated nor softened — because a reading that hides them would be reading a legend, not a man. Dates and quotations follow the conventional scholarship; the verification flags are observed in the notes.*

## The test

Joan tested the holographic content principle in a life held true under the maximum pressure, and the whole pattern was legible in it. Franklin tests it the other way. If the principle holds — if the whole is recoverable from any part — then it must hold not only in the rare life pressed to the fire but in the ordinary long one, lived in public over eighty-four years, with real moral failures left in plain view. A reading that only worked on saints would not be a reading; it would be a flattery. So this one keeps the failings in, and asks whether the framework's shape is still legible across a life that contained them — and whether the right measure of such a life is its perfection, which no life has, or its *fruit*, which is the thing the tradition said to judge by: *by their fruits ye shall know them* (Matthew 7:16). Franklin's life is read below in seven notes, and the virtue that runs through all of them is the plainest of the working virtues: diligence.

## 1. Do — Boston

The tonic is a candlemaker's shop. Benjamin Franklin was born in Boston in January 1706, the fifteenth child and youngest son of Josiah Franklin, a tallow chandler who boiled fat into soap and candles, and he had about two years of formal schooling before he was put to work. This is the ground note, and like Joan's it is deliberately low: no Latin, no university, no station, a tradesman's boy among a houseful of children. Everything the life becomes, it becomes by application from this floor. The *Do* is a boy dipping candles who taught himself to read by lamplight.

## 2. Re — the runaway and the project

The second note is the thing received and taken up — and for Franklin it is not a voice from heaven but a resolution, which is its own kind of reception: the conviction that a person can *make* himself by method. Apprenticed to his brother James's print shop, he read everything he could reach, broke the indenture, and ran to Philadelphia in October 1723, seventeen years old, with almost nothing. And there he conceived what he called, without irony, "the bold and arduous project of arriving at moral perfection": thirteen named virtues, Industry among them, tracked in a little book with a column for each day, a black mark set against every failure. He called his mistakes *errata*, a printer's word for the slips that get corrected in the next edition. The Re note is the principle the whole life will hold: the empirical method, turned first of all on himself — diligence as a discipline, the soul kept like a ledger.

## 3. Mi — Philadelphia, building

The third note is motion, and Franklin's is the motion of relentless construction. The printer established himself; *Poor Richard's Almanack* began for 1733, packed with the maxims of industry the whole world would later quote; and around him he built the civic furniture of a city almost single-handed — the Junto (1727), the Library Company (1731), the Union Fire Company (1736), the American Philosophical Society (1743), the academy that became a university (1749), the Pennsylvania Hospital (1751). This is diligence pushed outward into the world: a man who, finding a thing missing, simply organized it into being. But the boy from the candle shop could rise only so far on his own labor. The next level was not his to grant himself.

### Mi–Fa — the first shock: the world receives him

Here is the first interval, and it falls where the framework says it must — the place the ascent cannot continue on its own momentum and requires an input from outside. In 1748 Franklin's diligence had made him rich enough to retire from the shop, taking a partner to run it; that bought him time. But the crossing from a provincial tradesman to a man of consequence in the wider world was not a thing his industry could confer on itself. It had to be granted from outside — and it was, when the learned world received his electrical work: the Copley Medal in 1753, and election to the Royal Society in 1756, a self-made colonial printer admitted to the company of natural philosophers who would never have let him in on his birth. That external reception is the Mi–Fa shock. With it, the local builder crosses onto the world's stage.

## 4. Fa — electricity

The fourth note is the deed, and it is the one the world remembers: the kite raised into the storm in 1752, the single-fluid theory, the words *positive* and *negative* given to the science, and the lightning rod — a piece of applied mathematics and physics that took the most terrifying force in the sky and made it harmless on a wire. This is Fa in its exact sense, the principle in action proving itself in the world. And it carries the mark the framework prizes: he gave it away. Offered a patent on his stove, he refused it, on the ground that "as we enjoy great advantages from the inventions of others, we should be glad of an opportunity to serve others by any invention of ours; and this we should do freely and generously." The fruit of the diligence was handed to everyone, unpriced. The candlemaker's son had become the man who pulled the lightning out of the air and asked nothing for it.

## 5. Sol — the founding of a nation

The fifth note is the summit, and Franklin's diligence found its largest possible field: a nation. He sat on the committee that drafted the Declaration and signed it in 1776; he sailed to France and, by patience and charm and unrelenting work, secured the Treaty of Alliance of 1778 that made American victory possible; and at eighty-one he sat in the Constitutional Convention as its elder. The boy with two years of schooling helped make a republic and bind it to the only ally that could save it. This is the high, clear note — the method applied to the founding of a country, and the founding accomplished.

## 6. La — the errata

The sixth note descends, as the sixth note must, and here the reading keeps its promise not to look away. The public ledger was magnificent; the private one carried real debts, and Franklin, who marked his faults in a book, would not have wanted them hidden. He held enslaved people in his household for much of his life — never many, a domestic holding of one or two at a time, but real human beings owned, and for decades he printed advertisements for the sale of others in his newspaper and profited by them. He left his wife, Deborah, for years at a stretch; he was in London when she died in 1774, and they had not seen each other in a decade. He broke irreparably with his son William — his acknowledged son, born out of wedlock, who stayed loyal to the Crown — and never truly reconciled, leaving him almost nothing. He confessed in his own Autobiography that of all the virtues, humility was the one he could never master: he could perhaps subdue the appearance of pride, never the thing itself, "for even if I could conceive that I had completely overcome it, I should probably be proud of my humility." These are not footnotes to the life; they are part of it, and they are named here at full weight. The man who measured his soul daily did not come out clean. No one does. That is the whole reason the verdict cannot be perfection.

### Si–Do — the shock prepared

The descent runs toward the one crossing no diligence can make from inside: the end of the life itself, and what, if anything, the life amounts to past it. That crossing the man cannot perform; it waits in the seventh note and beyond it.

## 7. Si — the last labor, and the correction

The seventh note is the final diligence, worked under the weight of age and mortality, and it is where the deepest erratum is corrected. Too weak at the Convention to read his own closing speech, he had it read aloud for him — a plea that each delegate "doubt a little of his own infallibility" and sign the imperfect thing anyway, the exact humility he had confessed he lacked, reached for at the end. And in his last years the man who had owned and advertised slaves turned the whole of his remaining strength against slavery: he became president of the Pennsylvania Abolition Society, signed a petition to Congress for abolition in February 1790, and — three weeks before his death, in March 1790, writing under a pseudonym — published a satire of devastating wit, putting the exact pro-slavery arguments then being made in Congress into the mouth of an Algerian potentate defending the enslavement of Christians, so that the reader who had nodded along heard his own logic and recoiled. That satire is Franklin's version of Joan's testimony: the brilliance that springs the trap with logic, the mind at its sharpest in the service of the truth, deployed at the very end against his own oldest sin. The diligence held to the last, and it bent itself, finally, toward the correction of its author. He died on 17 April 1790, eighty-four years old; some twenty thousand people came to bury him.

## Do — the fruit

And the octave closes on its tonic, an octave up, by the only measure a flawed life can honestly be given: the fruit. The library still lends; the philosophical society still meets; the university still teaches; the hospital still heals; the postal system he organized still carries the mail; the rods he would not patent still stand on the roofs and take the lightning down to the ground; the republic he helped found endures; the abolition he came to late advanced after him, and his own will freed the enslaved he still held. A man does not have to be clean for his tree to bear. *By their fruits ye shall know them* — not by their perfection, which is given to no one, but by what grows from the life and outlasts it. Franklin's errata are real and are not erased by the harvest. But the harvest is real too, and it is enormous, and it is still feeding people who never knew his name. That is the resolving *Do*: the diligent, imperfect man, read by his fruit.

## What the reading shows

Set the seven notes side by side and the framework's whole shape is again there, in one long ordinary public life: the low tonic, the principle of diligence received and turned first on the self, the building outward, the first shock crossed by a recognition the man could not grant himself, the deed given freely, the summit of a founded nation, the honest descent into the errata, and the last labor that corrected the worst of them — with the second shock, death, opening onto a fruit the man could not himself secure. The two interval-shocks fall at their structural positions; the virtue runs unbroken through every note; and the science-and-theology of La is lived as one thing — the same empirical, methodical, self-correcting diligence applied to the lightning and to the soul, the ledger of the virtues a small foreshadow of measuring a life's proximity to the good over its whole course. The holographic content principle predicted the whole would be findable in a part. It is findable here, in a part with stains on it — which is the sterner and more useful confirmation, because it shows the reading does not require a saint. It requires only a life, read honestly, by its fruit.

His life is structured as a seven-note octave (Do Boston · Re the runaway and the project · Mi Philadelphia building · [Mi–Fa: the world receives him, Copley 1753 / FRS 1756] · Fa electricity, the gift given freely · Sol the founding · La the errata · Si the last labor and the abolition correction · [Si–Do: death] · Do the fruit), the two shocks at their positions. The failings are stated at full weight and kept fair: a small but real and decades-long household slaveholding plus slave-sale advertising (with a late, genuine abolitionism and manumission in his will); the long neglect of Deborah Read (last seen 1764; died 1774 in his absence); the unreconciled estrangement from his Loyalist son William; the documented French relationships (Brillon, Helvétius) which were flirtations rebuffed, not proven affairs; and the vanity he confessed he never conquered.
