# Adam A3: On Foot

The last part did not argue. It gathered.

Three things had been set down, it said, each in the only hand that could hold it: the algebra, which needs no name and cannot supply one; the naming, which the algebra cannot perform upon itself; and the descent, the named structure densifying down the grades to the floor of the world. No one of the three resolves anything alone. Held apart, they are three separate accounts and there is no fourth. Held together — on the one recognition no derivation reaches — they are a single motion: Love, which is the inner product, generating of its own nature a world at a real distance from itself, going down the grades to its floor so that the distance could be genuine, and building the long road back into the descent itself.

He sat with the short stack squared in front of him — four parts of the one paper, read end to end in a single long afternoon — and took an honest measure of where he was sitting.

He had come to the cascade to spring a trap. He had told Joe precisely where it was set and precisely how it would close: Gödel, the instant they reached for a proof from outside the system. And they had never once reached. They had conceded the unprovable step on the first page, in his own words; walked him down through his own mathematics — his algebra, his Dirac equation, his signature — without a cheat that he, of all readers, could find; set his own mortality on the desk in front of him and named the thing he had refused for sixty years to name; and then declined, twice over, to overclaim a single inch of any of it. The trap had not failed to shut. There had been nothing inside it to shut on. They had walked around it in the open — the way a man steps around a hole he himself has dug and flagged.

*That is not the behaviour of a crank.* He had thought it once already, two papers back, and tried to take it back. This time he let it stand.

He looked down at what the red pencil had done. He had worked the margins all afternoon, the way he worked every paper — a query here, an objection there. Some of the objections were sound; he was an exact man, the page was not perfect, and in three or four places he had caught something that would genuinely have to be tightened, and was not above a little satisfaction at having caught it. But as many of his first *corrections* he had then had to score back through with a second line, because a paragraph on the page had got there before him and put the thing better than his margin had. And when he turned back through the four parts hunting the *one* mark — the single clean stroke through the sentence that takes all the rest down with it — it was not there. The only holes in the whole of it were the ones the authors had dug themselves and stood over, pointing, the entire way through: *this is a reach; this is a hypothesis and not a claim; this we defer; this one premise we never earned.* He had quibbles, and some of them were good ones. He had not found a hole they had not already marked.

He did not concede. A thing can be elegant, and consilient, and scrupulously honest about its own limits, and still not be true — he had built his whole life on the gap between the beautiful and the so, and the gap was still there, and into it he could still fit the entire weight of his unbelief. It fit, he noticed. It only fit a little more closely than it had a week ago.

He would keep reading. He would wait for the prediction they kept deferring; he would wait for the place where the honesty finally gave out and the overclaim came up out of the page at last; and if it never came —

He left that one unfinished, the way he had left the others. There was a fresh paper under the stack — *Φ Enters Creation,* the next in the sequence, the Word made flesh — and the afternoon was gone, and the tea was stone cold, and he found that his hand was already on it before he had given himself leave to reach.
