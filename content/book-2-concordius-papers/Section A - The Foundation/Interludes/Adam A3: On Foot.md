# Adam A3: On Foot

Adam sat down with the fourth paper square in front of him and the red pencil already in his hand, and for the first time since the whole business began he found he was looking forward to it. He had told Joe exactly where the wall was. He had said *you can't get there from here* — that no single road, not the mathematics, not the theology, not the physics, crosses the line at the beginning, and that the moment anyone tried to bind the three into one proof that stood outside all three and pointed back at the ground of them, Gödel would have him by the throat. He had set the trap with his own hands. This was the paper that would walk into it.

It did not walk into it. It walked up to it, laid a flat hand against it, named it, and stopped.

He read the opening three times. *The structure is derivable; the name is not.* They said it plainly, in the first column, before they asked him for a thing: that whether the three names were one name was the single matter no derivation could reach, because the naming was a step no proof performs — *the recognition is an act no proof performs.* They were not going to try to prove it. They were going to ask him to take it, in the open, as a step taken on foot — and they said as much, and they said the science could keep everything it already had. *Nothing past this edge asks for any of that science back.*

He set the pencil down a moment, because the charge he had loaded the gun with was the round they had just handed back to him. He had told Joe they would have to claim more than a guess or a metaphor, and that the instant they did, he had them. And here, at the head of the paper, was the sentence conceding it could not be proved — calling the crossing a step, and not a theorem.

*That was my objection.* He had stood at the seminary door as a young man and said almost exactly this, in almost these words: that there is a bridge here a man has to walk on his own feet, and that he would not dignify the walking with the word *proof.* They had taken his objection and set it at the top of the page as their own first principle, and then walked on past it — as though a man might simply *take the step,* in good conscience, and look around at what stood on the other side.

"Clever," he said, to no one. "That is very clever." It was not quite a compliment. It was the sound a man makes when the door he had braced to slam is found standing open for him.

So he went down into the mathematics, where he could not be charmed, only convinced or not. And there the afternoon went strange on him.

The algebra was Cl(3,0) — three generators, positive-definite, associative — and they did not *choose* it; they argued it forced, one condition per Person, and at a single sitting he could not put his finger on the fourth algebra that would have served as well. Very well. Grant the toy its three axes. Then the descent, level by level, and at the floor of it the complement — the bare negation any definite selection leaves behind it, one undifferentiated −1 — coming into the structure as a single new generator, e₀² = −1, and Cl(3,0) becoming Cl(3,1).

And Cl(3,1) he knew the way he knew his own staircase in the dark. It is Hestenes' spacetime algebra. Write it out and the Dirac equation is sitting in it — *his* equation, the one he had lived inside for the best fifteen years of his working life. E = mc² drops out of it at rest, as it has to. And the Lorentzian signature — the three-and-one, the +,+,+,− that every physicist since Minkowski has simply *written down* because the world will not be talked out of it — they were claiming as the bookkeeping of a choice and its shadow: three Persons, no anti-Persons; therefore three positive generators and one negative; therefore one dimension of time, and not three. He read it twice looking for the sleight of hand — the moment the conjurer's hand goes into the hat with the rabbit already in his fist. He knew every way that trick is worked; he had failed undergraduates for it. *You can dress any equation a man already knows in a fresh notation and call the notation a discovery.* Retrodiction. The oldest cheat in mathematical physics: walk backward from the answer and announce a derivation.

The trouble was that the algebra had been nailed down three pages earlier, by the counting of the Persons, before one fact of physics was let into the room — and the signature came *out* of it, not *into* it. On one pass he could not locate where the answer had been buried. That did not mean it wasn't buried there. It meant he would have to go through the thing again, slower, and he knew already that he would.

He sat back. *Retrodiction.* That was the word, and the word was also the defence, and the defence still held — *barely,* he made himself add, which was not an admission he had expected to be making at his own desk. Every result in the paper was a walk backward to an equation already on the books. Dirac was Dirac in 1928. Friedmann was Friedmann in 1922. The sky was flat before these people drew their first circle. A framework that returns what we already possess has done a parlour trick, however graceful; it has not told us a single thing we did not already hold. *Show me one number I don't already own,* he told the empty room, *and I will sit up straight.*

And the paper, two pages on and very nearly in passing, offered him one. The speed of light, it said, ought to *drift* — that c is not a postulate but an output, a coupling whose value rides a structural ratio that has itself been shifting since the beginning, so that the constant against which every clock and rule is now set may not have held constant at all. It even pointed at the old scatter in the early determinations, and the 1983 redefinition that fixed c *by fiat* and so made the question unaskable in the standard units. *There.* That was not retrodiction. That was a neck laid on a block — a thing the universe is free to prove false.

Except that they would not bring the blade down. *A working hypothesis, not a claim,* said the parenthesis; the actual profile, the actual figure, *deferred to a later paper.* He almost laughed. They had walked him the whole way to the one spot he had demanded they stand — the spot where a clever man either says a falsifiable thing or admits that he cannot — and then declined, courteously, in a footnote, to stand on it yet. Twice now in a single afternoon: the proof they would not claim, and the prediction they would not yet make. A quarry harder to corner than any he had read, precisely because he would not be where the knife was.

He looked at the clock and was mildly affronted by it. The tea at his elbow had gone stone cold an hour since and he had not touched it. He had come to this paper to shut a trap, and had spent the afternoon discovering instead that the animal he was hunting had read the same field guide he had, and walked — deliberately, and in plain sight — around every snare set in it.

That was not the conduct of a crank. He sat with the fact a while, and took no pleasure in it.

Then he squared the pages and laid them on the short stack with the other three, and reached for the next — because there was no further use pretending he would not read it, and because somewhere under the irritation, in a place he would not have shown to Joe and could only just admit to himself, the hunt had begun to feel as though it might not be entirely his.
