# Adam A2B: A Formula for Love

He had meant to read the whole thing through at one sitting and grade it at the end. He got as far as the first part and had to stop.

It opened by taking his own knife out of his hand. The thing he had told Joe was the fatal flaw — that you cannot *derive* the Name, that the crossing from a structure to a God is a step a man takes on his own two feet and not a theorem — they had set down at the head of the page, in very nearly his words, and called it the first principle of everything that followed. *The structure is derivable; the name is not.* They were not going to try to prove it. They had conceded, on the first page, the precise point he had spent a week sharpening himself against, and walked on past it before he could bring the blade down on anything.

He read the derivation of the algebra twice. Three generators, one for each Person; positive-definite squaring, from the Father; associativity, from the Son. Cl(3,0) — a Clifford algebra, an object he had taught to graduate students, falling out of the doctrine of the Trinity as though it had been folded up inside it the whole time. He sat looking at their little table of seven elements — three singles, three pairs, the one whole — and could not make the count come out wrong. Two to the third, less one. Seven. *They have derived a Clifford algebra from the Athanasian Creed.* It ought to have been absurd. He wrote *absurd?* in the margin, and did not underline it, because he found he was no longer certain.

And then they did the thing that made him put the pencil down altogether.

They took the inner product — ⟨·,·⟩, the operation he had spent fifty years evaluating, the most ordinary tool in the whole bag, the thing he could compute in his sleep and had — and identified it, formally, with *love.* God is love, says the first letter of John; the Father is the inner product, said the paper before this one; therefore the inner product is what love, stated exactly, *is.* And then, without raising its voice: that to exist at all is to be evaluated by it — so that a thing is, in the most literal structural sense the words can carry, *loved into being at every moment it continues to be.*

He was quiet for some time.

He had spent the first half of his life learning to compute that bracket, and the second half declining to say aloud the word they had now set it equal to. To find the two halves of his own life written onto a single line, as a single object, by a stranger, inside a footnoted argument he could not at one reading fault — that was not a thing he had been prepared for over a cup of tea on an ordinary afternoon.

"No," he said, but there was not much behind it. The definition was elegant, and elegance is not truth; no one alive knew the difference better than he did; he had made a career of the gap between them. But he noticed that he had now read the page three times without locating the spot where the elegance cheats, and that was not how long it usually took him to find it.

He turned to the second part. They were leaving the algebra now and going down into the physics — into *his* country, onto ground he owned outright. Good. Let them come and try it on the Dirac equation. The charm would not survive the contact.
